## Executive Summary

A technology services client was ramping up for a new enterprise engagement that required 40 hires across four skill areas within 12 weeks. That kind of volume breaks down fast if every requisition gets treated the same way, and that's roughly what was happening when I stepped in. Requisitions were opening one at a time, sourcing was starting from zero on each one, and there was no shared view of which roles were actually blocking delivery versus which had room to move. I built a workforce segmentation model, prioritized 14 delivery-critical roles for immediate sourcing, and started proactive talent pooling on the remaining 26 before they were formally approved. By week 12, 33 of the 40 roles had closed, with all 14 critical roles filled by week 9.

---

## Business Background

The client was scaling technology delivery to support a new enterprise engagement, with hiring spread across backend development, cloud engineering, data platforms, and application support. Recruiter effort was high going in, requisitions were getting attention, and the numbers still weren't adding up. When hiring at this scale gets treated as 40 separate individual searches instead of one coordinated plan, effort and outcome tend to drift apart. That gap is what I was brought in to close.

---

## Hiring Challenge

The core problem was prioritization, or the lack of it. Some of the 40 roles were tied directly to go-live milestones and needed to close within weeks. Others had a three to four month runway. Both were being sourced with the same urgency, which meant recruiter time was getting spread evenly across roles that didn't deserve equal attention.

Market conditions made this worse. Cloud engineering candidates in particular were fielding two or three offers at once, and notice periods across the group averaged 45 to 60 days, which meant timelines needed to account for a joining lag that wasn't being planned for anywhere.

---

## Objectives

- Segment the 40 roles by delivery urgency instead of treating every requisition the same
- Get the 14 most delivery-critical roles into active sourcing within the first two weeks
- Build talent pools for the remaining roles ahead of formal requisition approval
- Set up a weekly hiring governance review with business and delivery stakeholders
- Track pipeline health and joining risk across all 40 roles from a single view

## Approach & Methodology

### Workforce Planning and Requirement Prioritization

The first move was splitting the 40 roles into two tiers. Fourteen were classified delivery-critical, tied to a go-live date within 8 weeks. The remaining 26 had longer lead time and went into a talent pooling track instead of active sourcing straight away. This alone changed how recruiter time got allocated, since it made clear where the next two weeks of effort actually needed to go.

### Talent Strategy and Market Mapping

For the 26 longer-lead roles, I started building talent pools before those requisitions were even formally opened, mapping candidates across the relevant skill areas so sourcing wouldn't be starting cold once approval came through. Cloud engineering needed the earliest start given how tight that market was running. Application support roles had a much deeper bench and could wait.

### Recruitment Intelligence Used

Market data showed cloud engineering and data platform candidates responding best to passive outreach and referral channels, while backend and application support roles moved faster through standard job board sourcing with quick screening turnaround. Applying one sourcing playbook across all four skill areas would have wasted effort on the ones that didn't need it.

---

## Execution

Execution opened with a prioritization session against the delivery timeline, which is where the 14 critical roles got identified and locked as the immediate focus. A single hiring tracker covered all 40 requisitions, showing stage, days-in-stage, and joining risk in one place instead of scattered across individual recruiter spreadsheets.

Weekly governance reviews with delivery and business stakeholders caught problems early. Two cloud engineering roles were flagged in week 4 as at risk due to thin candidate availability, which gave enough runway to widen sourcing before it became a week 10 crisis.

Candidate engagement didn't stop once someone accepted an offer. With average notice periods running 45 to 60 days, staying in regular contact through that gap mattered almost as much as the initial close, particularly for candidates who'd already turned down a competing offer to join.

---

## Challenges Faced During Execution

Balancing speed against quality was constant. Business stakeholders wanted every critical role closed immediately, and holding the line on technical screening standards took ongoing, sometimes uncomfortable conversation, especially in week 6 when two business leaders pushed to skip a screening round on a cloud engineering candidate who ultimately wasn't a fit anyway.

Requirements also shifted mid-project. Two roles originally scoped as longer-lead got reclassified as critical in week 5 after a scope change on the client side, which meant re-running part of the prioritization exercise. And for niche cloud skills specifically, even early talent pooling couldn't manufacture supply that didn't exist. Three of the seven remaining open roles at week 12 were in that exact skill area, and they closed in the following weeks rather than within the original 12-week window.

## Results & Business Impact

| Metric | Before | After |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Roles Closed by Week 12 | 0 of 40 | 33 of 40 |
| Critical Roles (14) Closed | 0 | 14 by Week 9 |
| Hiring Visibility | Requisition-level, siloed by recruiter | Single tracker across all 40 roles |
| Average Notice-Period Planning | Not factored into timelines | Built into joining forecasts |
| Pipeline Reviews | Ad hoc, inconsistent | Weekly governance cadence |

The 7 roles still open at week 12 were concentrated in the tightest cloud engineering skill segment, which the market mapping had flagged as a risk back in week 3. That's arguably the more useful outcome long term. Knowing which specific skill pocket was going to be the hard one, three months out, is what let leadership plan around it instead of being surprised by it.

---

## Key Takeaways

At this volume, hiring stops being about any single requisition and becomes a planning problem. The 14 critical roles closing on schedule wasn't down to sourcing harder. It came from deciding early which roles actually deserved that effort and which could wait.

The conversation with business stakeholders that mattered most wasn't "how fast can you close this," it was "which of these 40 roles will actually stop the project if they're late." Once that was answered honestly, prioritizing recruiter time got a lot easier.
