## Executive Summary

Six technology requisitions across three business units were open at the same time, and all six were aging past their target close date. Sourcing wasn't the bottleneck. The applicant tracking system showed a healthy number of candidates entering the pipeline every week. The real issue was pipeline velocity between the interview and offer stages, where strong candidates were sitting too long and eventually going cold or accepting a competing offer. Over eight weeks I ran a full funnel analysis, reworked the screening criteria with each hiring manager, and set a hard turnaround target on interview feedback. Screening-to-interview conversion improved by 30 percent, feedback turnaround dropped from 5 days to 2, and offer acceptance rate moved from 60 percent to 82 percent.

---

## Business Background

The organization was scaling technology delivery across three business units at once, and req volume reflected it. Recruiter activity metrics looked strong on paper, sourcing numbers were healthy, and yet requisitions kept slipping past their agreed time-to-fill targets. That gap between recruiter effort and actual close rate is usually a sign that the funnel itself, not sourcing volume, needs attention.

---

## Hiring Challenge

Pulling the funnel data by stage made the picture clear. Source-to-screen and screen-to-interview conversion were both within normal range for technology roles. The drop happened between interview and offer. A few root causes kept showing up. Screening criteria did not fully match what hiring managers actually wanted at the final round, so recruiters were sometimes advancing profiles that looked right on paper but weren't a fit in practice. Interview feedback was routinely taking close to a week to come back from panel members, which gave strong candidates enough time to accept a counter-offer or a competing offer elsewhere. On top of that, each business unit was tracking its own requisitions independently, so there was no shared view of req aging or drop-off rate across the portfolio.

None of this shows up if the only metric being watched is candidates sourced per week.

---

## Objectives

- Identify the exact stage in the funnel where conversion was breaking down
- Align recruiter screening criteria with actual hiring manager expectations at the panel level
- Reduce interview feedback turnaround time (TAT) to prevent candidate drop-off
- Improve offer acceptance rate by addressing decision speed rather than sweetening comp
- Build one shared view of req status and funnel health across all six requisitions

## Approach & Methodology

### Recruitment Funnel Analysis Conducted

I mapped the funnel across five stages: sourced, screened, interviewed, offered, joined, and pulled conversion rates between each one rather than relying on overall time-to-fill as the only signal. That stage-by-stage view is what surfaced the real problem. Roughly 4 in 10 candidates who reached final interview never received an offer in time to matter, mostly because panel feedback sat unactioned in the ATS for days at a stretch.

### Process Optimization Strategy

With the bottleneck identified, the fix centered on removing friction at the interview-to-offer stage specifically, rather than adding new steps upstream. I worked with each hiring manager individually to define what a strong candidate actually looked like for their team at final round, since generic competency frameworks weren't catching the real evaluation criteria being used informally. Interview panels agreed to a 48-hour feedback turnaround, and I built a single req tracker showing stage, days-in-stage, and flagged risk for all six requisitions in one place.

### Recruitment Intelligence Used

The funnel data made one pattern obvious: offer stage delay, not candidate quality or interest, was the primary driver of lost hires. Candidates were not dropping out due to disengagement. They were accepting other offers while still waiting on feedback from us. That shifted the framing of the entire initiative from sourcing more candidates to protecting pipeline velocity on the candidates already in play.

---

## Execution

The first two weeks were largely diagnostic, pulling stage-level conversion data from the ATS and cross-checking it against what hiring managers felt informally about where things were sticking. From week three, the revised screening criteria rolled out one business unit at a time instead of across all six requisitions simultaneously, mainly so the impact of the change could be measured before scaling it further.

The shared req tracker became the operating tool for the rest of the project. It was intentionally simple: current stage, days-in-stage, and any flagged risk, updated weekly. That single artifact cut down a significant amount of status-chasing between recruiters, hiring managers, and delivery leadership.

---

## Challenges Faced During Execution

The harder part of this project was not the analysis itself. It was convincing two of the six hiring managers that increasing sourcing volume was not the fix, since that had been the default response to slow requisitions for a long time. Panel availability was a real constraint too. Committing to a 48-hour feedback window is easy to agree to and harder to consistently deliver when panel members are also carrying delivery workload. A few interview rounds still ran past the target window, and one requisition still lost a strong, final-round candidate to a competing offer even after the new process was in place. The redesign reduced how often that happened. It did not eliminate it entirely.

## Results & Business Impact

| Metric | Before | After |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Screening-to-Interview Conversion | Baseline | Up 30% |
| Interview Feedback Turnaround (TAT) | 5 Days | 2 Days |
| Offer Acceptance Rate | 60% | 82% |
| Candidates Lost Post-Interview | Roughly 4 in 10 | Roughly 1 in 10 |
| Funnel Visibility Across Requisitions | Fragmented by business unit | Single shared req tracker |

The larger shift was in what the team was optimizing for day to day. Once feedback turnaround became the metric everyone actively watched, the rest of the funnel, including offer acceptance, improved as a downstream effect rather than requiring separate intervention.

---

## Key Takeaways

The clearest lesson from this project was that a healthy top-of-funnel can mask a broken middle. The instinctive response to a slow requisition is almost always to source harder, and in this case that instinct was pointed at the wrong stage entirely. The real cost was sitting in feedback turnaround, not candidate scarcity.

The harder part of this kind of initiative is rarely the data work. It's shifting a team away from an activity that feels productive, like more sourcing, toward one that actually moves the needle, like faster panel decisions.
